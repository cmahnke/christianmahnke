In 2017, I worked a lot of overtime and so spent half the spring in [Levinschen Park](https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levinscher_Park)...

<!--more-->

Ever since then, I’ve been wondering whether it might just be a whale’s lower jaw lying there, without anyone having noticed.

I was reminded of this again during the week and took a few photos.

Now only half of the object remains. However, on Google Maps I found it looking just as I remember it:

* [Image from July 2021](https://maps.app.goo.gl/v9HepKjN1ujBAq4EA). On the right-hand edge of the picture.
* [Image from June 2022](https://maps.app.goo.gl/Dnhx4ZmbtxEWKUvHA). Behind the red climbing frame on the right.

I’ve seen structures like this before, for example in North Frisia. Here you can see a whale bone portal at the Dr Carl Häberlin Frisian Museum in Wyk auf Föhr.

{{< figure src="./Friesenmuseum.jpeg" alt="Portal of the Dr Carl Häberlin Frisian Museum in Wyk auf Föhr" caption="Image by [amras_wi](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Friesenmuseum.JPG), public domain" >}}

Wikimedia Commons contains many [comparative images of arches made from baleen](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:Whale_bone_arches) (more specifically: the lower jaws of baleen whales).

On closer inspection, even more details become apparent:
* The material is fibrous in the upper layers and porous in the deeper layers.
* Organic-looking, upward-facing openings are visible on the front, which are thought to be blood vessels.

The biologist and photographer Dirk Pfuhl regularly documents the wildlife of [Levinscher Park](http://stadt-natur-wildnis.de/category/lebensraeume/levinscher-park-goettingen/) in Göttingen. As I worked with him over 20 years ago, I wrote to him to ask for his opinion.
